# Corn stover cellulosic ethanol process

Corn stover is the stalks, leaves, and cobs left after a corn harvest. It is a
lignocellulosic (cellulosic) feedstock, meaning its sugars are locked in
cellulose and hemicellulose rather than being freely available. Converting it to
ethanol is a "second-generation" process and follows the NREL biochemical
design:

- Pretreatment: dilute-acid pretreatment breaks down the biomass structure so
  enzymes can access the cellulose.
- Enzymatic saccharification: enzymes hydrolyze cellulose into glucose and
  hemicellulose into xylose.
- Co-fermentation: organisms ferment both glucose and xylose into ethanol. The
  glucose-to-ethanol conversion (often called fermentation efficiency) and the
  xylose-to-ethanol conversion are key performance parameters.
- Product recovery: distillation and dehydration purify the ethanol.
- Cogeneration: lignin and other residues are burned to produce steam and
  electricity for the plant.

Because cellulosic conversion is harder than fermenting simple sugars, glucose
and xylose conversion efficiencies strongly affect ethanol yield and therefore
the minimum ethanol selling price.

# Sugarcane (first-generation) ethanol process

Sugarcane ethanol is a "first-generation" process because the sugars are already
present as sucrose and are readily fermentable. The process is:

- Milling: the cane is crushed to extract sugar-rich juice; the fibrous residue
  is bagasse.
- Fermentation: yeast converts the sugars to ethanol. The fermentation
  efficiency sets how much of the sugar becomes ethanol.
- Distillation: the ethanol is concentrated and purified.
- Cogeneration: bagasse is burned to produce steam and electricity, often making
  the plant energy self-sufficient or a net exporter of power.

First-generation sugarcane ethanol is generally cheaper to produce than
cellulosic ethanol because it skips pretreatment and enzymatic hydrolysis.

# Lipidcane ethanol and biodiesel process

Lipidcane is sugarcane engineered to accumulate oil (lipids) in addition to
sugar. It enables a biorefinery that co-produces two fuels:

- Milling and oil extraction: the cane is crushed to release both sugar juice
  and oil.
- Fermentation: the sugars are fermented to ethanol, like conventional
  sugarcane.
- Transesterification: the extracted oil is converted to biodiesel.
- Cogeneration: bagasse is burned to provide steam and electricity.

Because the plant sells two products, the minimum ethanol selling price is
computed with biodiesel valued at its co-product price. Co-production can make
the ethanol economics look very favorable, since biodiesel revenue offsets cost.

# Fermentation efficiency and conversion

Fermentation efficiency (or conversion) is the fraction of available sugar that
the microorganisms turn into ethanol. Higher conversion means more ethanol from
the same feedstock and capital, which lowers the minimum ethanol selling price.
In the corn stover model, glucose and xylose conversions are set separately. In
the sugarcane model, a single fermentation efficiency governs glucose-to-ethanol
conversion.
